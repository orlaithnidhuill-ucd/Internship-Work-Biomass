# Learning Outcomes:
Upon completion of this module students should be able to:
•	Transfer space project management concepts and methods into a workplace environment;
•	Depending on the project, apply skills in, for example, report-writing, data analysis, coding, systems engineering, modelling and payload design, to address and solve previously unseen challenges;
•	Effectively and reliably participate in a team, understanding different roles and their associated responsibilities;
•	Illustrate the work undertaken in the form of a well-designed poster, accessible to a broad technical audience;
•	Verbally communicate the key elements of the project;
•	Justify decisions made and conclusions drawn about the project, when questioned;
•	Place the work within its context in a comprehensive written report.
•	This is a 30ECTS module meaning 500-600 hours of student effort, approx. equivalent to 40 hours per week for 12 weeks.

# Assessment Information
Three components contribute to the overall module grade:
- Written report (50%)
- Host supervisor's assessment (30%)
- Poster presentation and Q&A (20%)

## Poster
Instructions and resources for preparing your poster. The templates are to provide a basic structure and overall design aesthetic. However, you don't have to stick to the presentation format (e.g. the named section headings, 3 figures across the bottom etc) as shown if it doesn't work for your project.

### Poster Guidelines
There are lots of good guides to preparing effective posters, so please look at these, aswell as the templates provided, before you start thinking about making your poster. As usual, don’t leave this to the last minute.

### Key points:
•	Poster title, your name, and company/research group name should all be at the top of the poster  
•	You should use/adapt one of the supplied templates in this folder. You don’t have to keep strictly to this layout if it doesn’t suit you or your project.  
•	Use the company logo in addition to the UCD  
•	Important information should be readable from about 10 feet away  
•	Keep your title short to draw interest  
•	Word count should be 300 to 500 words (some guides advise fewer words - that can work too, depending on your topic and message)   
•	Keep text succinct and to the point  
•	Use bullets, numbering and headlines to make your ‘story’ flow easily for the reader  
•	Make effective use of graphics, flow charts, colours and fonts  
•	No fuzzy reproduction of images  
•	Ensure the layout is consistent, readable and visually appealing  
•	Include acknowledgments, making mention of your workplace supervisor  
•	Take accessibility into account (e.g. suitable fonts, colour schemes, size of font, see below for examples)  
•	Allow enough time to get feedback and sign-off from your internship supervisor

## Report
The outline listed below is for guidance only. There may not be an exact correspondence
between your particular project work and this format. The language used may differ between a research project and an industry one, but the basic structure will be similar. The focus should be on the work you have done during the internship. If you are not sure of anything, ask for help EARLY!

Your report should answer the following questions:
1. Why are you doing this? Who are you doing it with? [Introduction; Context for the
project; Overview of the research group or company’s activity] [1-2 pages]
2. What are other people doing in this area globally? [Summary of the ‘state of the art’;
competitors’ approaches] [1-2 pages]
3. Why is your way better? [Novelty of your project and its relevance to space; advancing
the state of the art (industry); advancing knowledge (research)] [2-3 pages]
4. What have you done? [e.g. Experiments; Software development; Analysis;
Computational work; Automation; QA; Testing; R&D] [5-7 pages]
5. How have you done it? [Set-up; procedures implemented; measurements;
equipment; coding; processes] [5-6 pages]
6. What did you find? [Results, challenges, re-work, revisions, errors, analysis, graphs] [2-3 pages]
7. What did you learn? [Conclusions, reflections, summary, success, goals achieved, next steps/future work] [1-2 pages]

Number of pages per topic is indicative only, but your report must be under 25 pages in total. You must emphasise the space context for your work. Even if you are developing software or doing something R&D related that may not be directly space connected, you need to explicitly address the space element in your report.

# Writing your Report
Do not leave writing the report until August. Think of it as a separate work-package that you need to be chipping away at throughout the project. Even if you begin by putting a template together and just keep adding bullet points, that will help you a lot. You might find it helpful to use a tool such as Pacemaker to figure out what writing approach works best for you. Documenting your work as you go takes the pain out of writing the final report.

# Structure and Layout of the Report
Follow the general standard layout to structure your report. The total length for the main
chapters (i.e. excluding title page, original authorship statement, acknowledgements,
abstract, table of contents, list of figures and tables, bibliography and appendices) should be 25 pages max, using 12pt Times Roman (or similar) font and approximately 2.5cm margin all around. Recommendation is to use LaTeX/Overleaf but this is not obligatory.

•	Title Page  
  o	UCD Crest, Internship Organisation Logo (if applicable), project title, degree award for which the report is being submitted; module title, your name, student number and date.  
•	Signed statement of original authorship page  
  o	 ‘I, the undersigned, declare that this submission is entirely my own work, in my own words, and that all sources used in its preparation, including AI, are fully acknowledged. It has not been submitted, in whole or in part, by me or anyone else, for the purpose of obtaining any other credit or grade. I have read and understood UCD’s policy on plagiarism.’ Add your signature (electronic is fine) and date to this statement.  
•	Acknowledgements  
  o	Words of thanks to all those who supported you in your internship.  
•	Abstract  
  o	Max 300 words summarising the key objectives and findings of your project and its relevance to space.  
•	Table of Contents  
•	List of Figures and Tables  
•	List of Acronyms  
•	Chapter 1 – Introduction.   
  o	If a research-based project, present the current state-of-the-art, and a statement of the research questions/problems addressed by your project, and their relevance to space. If an industry-based project, describe the business of the company, the services or products offered, and the global market in which the company/business operates.  
•	Chapter 2 - Theory (only include if relevant to your project)   
  o	An outline of the relevant theoretical background to your project.  
•	Chapter 3 - Observations / Methodology / Experimental Set-up.   
  o	A description of the approach employed in the internship, including an assessment of the suitability/limitations of this approach to address the problem in question.  
•	Chapter 4 – Results.   
  o	Present your results, data analysis and findings, including errors/uncertainties, where relevant.  
•	Chapter 5 – Discussion.   
  o	This gives your interpretation of the results and the implications.  
•	Chapter 6 - Conclusions.   
  o	A concise statement of your results and their implications within the field. It should also briefly outline the impact of the results in a broader context e.g. for the economy and/or society. Finally, this section should include recommendations, including future research directions.  

_In some cases, Chapters 4 and 5 can be merged, or Chapters 5 and 6. You can decide which structure works best for your project. You may not need a Theory chapter for example. Just make sure you are addressing questions 1-7 [above] within your report._  

•	Bibliography (apply the Harvard System of referencing)  
•	Appendix/Appendices.   
  o	This gives further information which you may wish to include, but which is too cumbersome to be included in the main body of the report without disrupting the flow of the text. For example, you may include long tables or multiple figures, computer scripts, or code written, during the internship or additional background information. Please note that the assessment is based primarily on the body of work presented within the 25 page limit.
